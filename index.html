The Project

From a broad, open ended question to a focused plan.

The flour category is mature. Growth does not come from selling everywhere at once, it comes from sharper focus, better mix, and disciplined execution. My job was to figure out where that focus belonged, and to prove it.

1 · Situation

A country on the move

The U.S. population keeps shifting south and west. The way the category had historically been served was built for a different decade, and the growth corridors had quietly drifted away from the legacy footprint.

2 · Problem

The old views hid the gaps

Looking at the business only by customer or by channel buried the geographic story. Whole pockets of opportunity were sitting in plain sight, just not in any report anyone was looking at.

3 · The Ask

Build a market first view

Create a clear read of where demand and growth really live, then turn it into a practical recommendation that works inside the resources the team already has.

The honest part

It did not come together in a straight line.

Here are the real bottlenecks I ran into, and what I did about them. This is the part I am proudest of, because solving these is what made the work usable.

1

The metrics did not agree with each other

Different reports measured volume and value in different ways, so two people could describe the same business and reach opposite conclusions. Before I could analyze anything, I had to standardize a single, consistent way to talk about volume, value, and profit quality. That cleanup became the foundation everything else stood on.

2

The data lived in a dozen places

Market data, internal performance, and field knowledge were scattered across separate systems and people's heads. I pulled third party market data, internal dashboards, and raw extracts together and reconciled them into one model, which meant a lot of careful, unglamorous matching to make sure the numbers actually lined up.

3

The numbers alone can be misleading

Early on, the analysis pointed at markets that looked great on paper but were not realistic to win. The fix was not more spreadsheets, it was conversations. Talking to the field separated the attractive markets from the achievable ones, and it genuinely changed my answer.

4

Learning an entire industry on the clock

I started with almost no flour or foodservice background. Getting fluent fast, in the products, the channels, the customers, and the economics, meant asking a lot of questions and earning time with busy people. The immersion was steep, but it is the reason the final recommendation sounded like it came from someone who knew the business.

Five lenses, one scoring model and a defensible answer.

Lens 1 · Circana & Nielsen DMA

Where is the biggest accessible market?

At 30%, it is tied for the highest weighted lens because it captures our current flour share, and whether it is an under or over-penetrated area.

So overall, this methodology of weighted lenses is based on operational reality, real field insights and a plethora of research instead of ranking markets on size alone.

Field Insights

The layer that made it real.

The data sized the outlook. The field told me how to actually go out and win. I followed up by building a living insights log so this knowledge would not disappear when the internship ended.

Cost to serve is everything

A market can look perfect on paper and still be unprofitable to reach. The field reframed several opportunities around freight and proximity, not just demand.

Product fit beats price

Across territories the same theme surfaced. Some opportunities needed a better product match, not a sharper price. That reframed what was realistic to chase.

The field changed the answer

Meeting with the full sales team turned a clean but theoretical analysis into a practical plan. The recommendation got better every time I talked to someone.

The Journey

Ten weeks, end to end.

This was more than a single project. It was a fast, full immersion into how a national sales organization really works, from out in the field to the leadership room.

Weeks 1 to 2 · Getting Oriented

Understanding foodservice and General Mills

I spent my first two weeks learning the company and the foodservice ingredients business from the ground up. The products, the channels, the customers, and the metrics the team lives by, plus how General Mills actually operates day to day.

Week 3 · Sales Immersion

Out in the field on live sales calls

I rode along on real sales calls with a customer account specialist and an account executive, across a range of channels including lodging, commercial, and non commercial operators. Seeing the business at the table changed how I read every number afterward.

Weeks 4 to 5 · Building the Foundation

Framing the question and the model

I reframed a broad prompt into something I could actually score. I standardized the metrics, chose the six lenses, and assembled the analytical workbook that everything else would be built on.

Weeks 6 to 8 · Stakeholder Engagement

Naional Sales Meeting

I ran 1:1 conversations across the FSI sales team and attended our National Sales Meeting (NSM). At NSM, I had the pleasure to expand my knowledge well beyond just selling in terms of FSI but also our Foodservice (FSD) team. Their real world perspective grounded the analysis, surfaced themes the data could not see, and sharpened the recommendation in ways a spreadsheet never would.

Weeks 8 to 9 · Synthesis & the Build

Turning analysis into a tool

I translated the model and the field intelligence into a phased, practical plan, and built the website in VS Code, paired with Codex, so the team could keep exploring the work long after I left.

Week 10 · The Panel

Presenting to leadership

The work ended where it was always headed, in front of senior leaders, with a full handoff kit so the thinking could keep moving. I left with a recommendation on the table and work I would put my name on anywhere.

What Changed

The improvements I am proudest of.

Beyond the recommendation itself, a few things I built will keep paying off after the internship.

A cleaner way to keep score

I standardized how volume, value, and profit quality get measured by metro for flour share. The team now has one consistent language instead of competing versions of the truth.

A reusable strategy tool

The scoring model and the website give the team a repeatable way to look at geographic focus, not a one time slide that gets forgotten.

Field knowledge that stays

The living insights log captures hard won field knowledge in one place, so it does not walk out the door when people move roles.

A focus, not a wish list

The biggest shift was moving the conversation from selling everywhere to a prioritized, defensible focus the team can actually execute.
